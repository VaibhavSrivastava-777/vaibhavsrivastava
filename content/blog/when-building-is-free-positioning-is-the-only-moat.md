title: "When Building is Free, Positioning is the Only Moat"
date: "2026-03-16"
category: "Product Strategy"
excerpt: "AI agents now let anyone ship a product in weeks. When building is cheap, markets flood with competing solutions — and buyers drown in choice. The scarce resource is no longer code. It's meaning."
---

<p>Not long ago, <em>Product Development</em> was the dominant conversation at every tech water cooler. Product-Market Fit was the North Star. Eric Ries' <em>Lean Startup</em> made it gospel: engineering hours are precious, and unless you're running disciplined Build-Measure-Learn cycles, you're failing your customers <em>and</em> your team. Dropbox didn't build a product first — they built a waitlist video and validated 75,000 signups before writing a line of production code. That was the gold standard.</p><p>That constraint is dissolving.</p><p><strong>The New Reality: Abundance of Code</strong></p><p>Tools like Cursor, GitHub Copilot, Bolt.new, and v0 by Vercel have fundamentally compressed the cost of building. GitHub reports that developers using Copilot complete tasks up to <strong>55% faster</strong>. Replit's AI agents can scaffold a functional web app in minutes. What took a 3-person team three months now takes one person three days.</p><p>We now have agents that can fill the role of developer, UI designer, UX researcher, QA tester, business analyst, and planner — with <em>you</em> as the central orchestrator. The Build-Measure-Learn loop hasn't just accelerated. It has become near-infinite.</p><p>The natural consequence? <strong>When building is cheap, the barrier to entry collapses.</strong> Product Hunt lists hundreds of new AI tools weekly. Buyers now face more choices, not fewer — which means their cost of evaluation goes up, their risk of picking the wrong thing goes up, and their need for a clear signal of <em>"this one is right for me"</em> goes up dramatically.</p><p><strong>Positioning is precisely that signal.</strong></p><p><strong>Three Things That Become Scarce When Code Is Abundant</strong></p><p><strong>1. Trust</strong></p><p>When 40 tools solve the same problem with similar AI stacks, buyers don't evaluate them all. They default to the one they've heard of, that sounds like it was built for them, and that carries social proof from peers they respect. Consider how Notion displaced Confluence not on features, but on the feeling of being built for <em>modern teams</em> — a positioning decision, not a technical one.</p><p><strong>2. Distribution</strong></p><p>A well-positioned product gets talked about in the right rooms. A generic one doesn't get talked about at all. Slack didn't win because it had better chat infrastructure than HipChat. It won because it was positioned as <em>where work happens</em> — a cultural identity, not a utility. In a crowded market, your position determines whether word-of-mouth travels or dies.</p><p><strong>3. Pricing Power</strong></p><p>Commoditized products get commoditized pricing. Linear charges a premium over free Jira alternatives not because its underlying technology is radically different, but because its positioning — <em>software built for speed and craftsmanship</em> — attracts buyers who self-identify with those values and pay accordingly. Perceived differentiation is economically equivalent to real differentiation.</p><p><strong>A Six-Step Framework for Positioning in the AI Era</strong></p><p>I follow a disciplined six-step sequence. The first five are research inputs. Step six is the only output. The most common mistake? Writing the positioning statement before doing the upstream work — which produces generic claims nobody believes.</p><p><strong>01 — Competitive Landscape</strong> Who do you <em>truly</em> compete with? Think beyond direct rivals. When Figma launched, Sketch was the obvious competitor — but the real alternative was "not designing collaboratively at all." That reframe changed everything about their GTM.</p><p><strong>02 — Customer Job</strong> What is the buyer actually hiring you to do? Functional, emotional, and social jobs all shape willingness to pay. Notion's customers aren't just organizing notes — many are signaling to peers that they are thoughtful, modern knowledge workers.</p><p><strong>03 — Unique Value</strong> What can you claim that competitors cannot credibly copy? If the claim could appear on a rival's website unchanged, it isn't sharp enough. "Powerful and easy to use" is not a claim. "The only CI/CD tool built for monorepos at scale" is.</p><p><strong>04 — Target Persona</strong> Who specifically loses sleep over the problem you solve — and who are you explicitly <em>not</em> for? Linear famously positioned against enterprise buyers. That exclusion made it the default for high-performance startup engineering teams. Exclusion is a feature of good positioning.</p><p><strong>05 — Proof &amp; Credibility</strong> Every assertion needs a proof architecture matched to the buyer's level of skepticism. G2 reviews, case studies, benchmark data, logos — each serves a different moment in the trust-building journey.</p><p><strong>06 — Positioning Statement</strong> Synthesize everything into a crisp internal north star — for whom, against what alternatives, and why you win — that drives all downstream GTM: messaging, pricing, sales narrative, content strategy.</p><p><strong>The Historical Parallel That Should Give You Confidence</strong></p><p>This has happened before. In the early 1990s, desktop publishing tools like Adobe PageMaker and QuarkXPress made graphic design accessible to anyone with a Mac. The prediction was that professional designers would be obsolete. What actually happened: mediocre design became noise, and the market <em>flew toward quality, taste, and clarity</em>. Designers who understood <em>for whom</em> and <em>for what purpose</em> — who had a point of view — thrived. The rest were commoditized.</p><p>AI IDEs are doing the same to software. The flight will be toward clarity, trust, and specificity. That is exactly what positioning delivers.</p><p><strong>Key Takeaways</strong></p><ul><li>AI coding tools have made building fast and cheap — which makes <em>differentiation</em>, not execution, the primary competitive battleground.</li><li>The three new scarce resources are <strong>trust</strong>, <strong>distribution</strong>, and <strong>pricing power</strong> — all of which are manufactured through positioning, not product.</li><li>The six-step positioning framework only works if steps 01–05 are completed honestly before step 06 is written. Sequence is the discipline.</li><li>The desktop publishing parallel is instructive: abundance of tooling historically triggers a <em>flight to quality and meaning</em>, not a race to the bottom.</li></ul><p><strong>When building is free, the only moat left is meaning — and positioning is how you manufacture meaning in the mind of the buyer.</strong></p><p><em>What's your experience?
